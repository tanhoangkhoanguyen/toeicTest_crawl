To: All directors
From: Reed Abelson
Date: September 19
Subject: Directors' meeting agenda

Dear Directors,

In response to changing publication trends, I offer the following agenda to discuss at the directors' meeting next Monday.

Our sales department reports that sale of the Fishing Monthly in print edition have been drastically declining over the past couple of years and have actually dropped by 40% this year. Since print publication rates are diminishing and electronic publication rates and rising, we also have to consider turning our sights on electronic publication.

There is statistical evidence that electronic publishing provides wider dissemination, and a number of journals have already established electronic versions or even moved entirely to electronic publication. Electronic publication will cut down production and shipping costs and attract more readers at a reduced price and with better accessibility. In addition, the government offers subsidies for green publishing to companies which adopt a publishing process to minimize the impact on the environment by using digital or print- on- demand technology.

Given these facts, I'd like us to start thinking about ways to promote Fishing Monthly's online edition and reduce the print edition by at least 50% next year. In order to cut down on print publishing, we might have to shut down some of our production plants. This surely is not an easy decision. However, given the current bus business climate, I feel these changes are absolutely essential to succeed in the coming year and beyond.

I look forward to discussing this and other issues involving the magazine with all of you on Monday.

Reed.

Oil spills at sea threaten animal life, plant life and other marine resources. Oil can also cause long-term environmental and economic damage to marine ecosystems near a spill. There are several ways to tackle oil spills at sea. The best method is to collect the oil and physically recover it from the water. However, the mechanical recovery of oil is not feasible in rough weather conditions. Secondly, the cost of oil cleanups by mechanical means is high when compared to chemical cleanup methods.

Today, the chemical dispersion of oil is one of the most favored ways to deal with offshore oil pollution. These chemicals contain surface active agents in a solvent base which are absorbed at the oil/water interface and lower interfacial tension. This facilitates the production of small oil droplets into the water column. Oil dispersed effectively into a larger volume of seawater is rapidly diluted by turbulence and its concentration is reduced soon to background levels.

Concern that chemical dispersants could be toxic to marine life has led to considerable caution in authorizing their use at spill sites. The newest generation of dispersants is less acutely toxic than earlier products, though misuse can lead to environmental hazards. Secondly, our dispersants satisfy the efficiency, stability and toxicity criteria and have received approval from the government.
